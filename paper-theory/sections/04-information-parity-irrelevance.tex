This section supplies the information-theoretic engine of the paper. It builds
the three quantities the spine needs (entropy, relative entropy, mutual
information), proves the data-processing inequality in the exact form the
irrelevance theorem consumes, identifies \emph{sufficiency} as the structural
condition under which the inequality is tight, and then defines
\emph{information parity} as that tightness condition. The negative half of
the thesis (\cref{sec:intro}) is, at this level, a single sentence: parity
makes the data-processing inequality an equality, so the information available
to an optimal predictor does not move with the representation. Making that
sentence a theorem about Bayes risk is deferred to T2-2; here we fix the
objects it quantifies over. We follow the standard treatments of
\citet{coverthomas2006} for the information quantities and rate--distortion,
and \citet{lehmanncasella1998} for sufficiency and the factorization theorem;
all objects are the measure-theoretic ones of \cref{sec:prelim}.

\subsection{Entropy, relative entropy, mutual information}
\label{subsec:infoquantities}

Throughout, $X \markov \rep(X) \markov Y$ and related Markov statements are
read against the joint law $\jlaw$ of \cref{ass:sbs}; all random elements live
on the common space $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\Prob)$. We state the discrete forms
(finite token alphabets and countable program syntax are the operative case
for \texttt{preprint-v1}); the definitions extend to the standard Borel
setting of \cref{sec:prelim} by taking suprema over finite measurable
partitions \citep[Ch.~8]{coverthomas2006}, and nothing downstream depends on
the continuous refinement.

\begin{definition}[Entropy and conditional entropy]
\label{def:entropy}
For a discrete random element $U$ with law $p_U$, the \emph{entropy} is
$\Ent{U} \defeq -\sum_{u} p_U(u)\log p_U(u)$, and for a pair $(U,V)$ the
\emph{conditional entropy} is
$\CondEnt{U}{V} \defeq -\sum_{u,v} p_{UV}(u,v)\log p_{U \given V}(u\given v)$,
with the convention $0\log 0 = 0$. Logarithms are natural; entropies are in
nats.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Relative entropy]
\label{def:kl}
For probability measures $\mu \ll \nu$ on a common space, the
\emph{relative entropy} (Kullback--Leibler divergence) is
$\KL{\mu}{\nu} \defeq \int \log\!\big(\rnd{\mu}{\nu}\big)\,\mathrm{d}\mu$,
with $\KL{\mu}{\nu} = +\infty$ when $\mu \not\ll \nu$. Gibbs' inequality gives
$\KL{\mu}{\nu} \ge 0$, with equality iff $\mu = \nu$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Mutual information]
\label{def:mi}
The \emph{mutual information} between $U$ and $V$ is the relative entropy
between their joint law and the product of the marginals,
\[
  \MI{U}{V} \defeq \KL{\law{(U,V)}}{\,\law{U}\otimes\law{V}\,}
            = \Ent{U} - \CondEnt{U}{V}
            = \Ent{V} - \CondEnt{V}{U}.
\]
By \cref{def:kl}, $\MI{U}{V} \ge 0$, with equality iff $U \indep V$. The
\emph{conditional mutual information} $\MI{U}{V \given W}$ is defined
analogously from the conditional laws, and $\MI{U}{V\given W}=0$ iff
$U \indep V \given W$.
\end{definition}

The one identity we use repeatedly is the \emph{chain rule}: for any $U,V,W$,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:chainrule}
  \MI{U}{(V,W)} \;=\; \MI{U}{V} \;+\; \MI{U}{W \given V},
\end{equation}
which follows by writing each term via \cref{def:entropy} and cancelling
\citep[Thm.~2.5.2]{coverthomas2006}.

\subsection{The data-processing inequality}
\label{subsec:dpi}

The inequality is stated for a Markov chain and then specialized to a
representation, which is the only instance the paper uses.

\begin{lemma}[Data-processing inequality]
\label{lem:dpi}
If $U \markov V \markov W$ is a Markov chain (i.e.\ $U \indep W \given V$),
then $\MI{U}{W} \le \MI{U}{V}$, with equality iff $U \markov W \markov V$ also
holds (i.e.\ $V$ carries no information about $U$ beyond what $W$ already does).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Expand $\MI{U}{(V,W)}$ by the chain rule \eqref{eq:chainrule} in the two
orders:
\[
  \MI{U}{V} + \MI{U}{W \given V}
  \;=\; \MI{U}{(V,W)} \;=\;
  \MI{U}{W} + \MI{U}{V \given W}.
\]
The Markov hypothesis $U \indep W \given V$ is exactly $\MI{U}{W\given V}=0$
(\cref{def:mi}), so $\MI{U}{V} = \MI{U}{W} + \MI{U}{V \given W}$. Since
$\MI{U}{V\given W}\ge 0$, we obtain $\MI{U}{V} \ge \MI{U}{W}$. Equality holds
iff $\MI{U}{V\given W}=0$, i.e.\ $U \indep V \given W$, which is the Markov
chain $U \markov W \markov V$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[A representation cannot create predictive information]
\label{cor:dpi-rep}
For any representation $\rep$ of \cref{def:rep}, $\rep(X)$ is a measurable
function of $X$, so $Y \markov X \markov \rep(X)$ is a Markov chain. Hence
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:dpi-rep}
  \MI{Y}{\rep(X)} \;\le\; \MI{Y}{X},
\end{equation}
with equality iff $Y \markov \rep(X) \markov X$, i.e.\ iff $\rep(X)$ retains
all of $X$'s information about $Y$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
$\rep(X)$ is $\sigalg{X}$-measurable, so it is conditionally independent of
$Y$ given $X$: $Y \indep \rep(X) \given X$, which is the chain
$Y \markov X \markov \rep(X)$. Apply \cref{lem:dpi} with
$(U,V,W)=(Y,X,\rep(X))$.
\end{proof}

\cref{cor:dpi-rep} is the formal content of \cref{rem:lossy} and of the
monotonicity noted after \cref{def:bayesrisk}: a representation can only
coarsen the input $\sigma$-algebra, hence can only weakly decrease the
information about $Y$. The equality case is the object the rest of the section
names.

\subsection{Sufficiency and the Fisher--Neyman factorization}
\label{subsec:sufficiency}

The equality case of \cref{cor:dpi-rep} is classical sufficiency, transported
from parameter estimation to prediction by reading $Y$ in the role of the
parameter.

\begin{definition}[Sufficiency for $Y$]
\label{def:sufficiency}
A representation $\rep$ is \emph{sufficient for $Y$} if
$Y \indep X \given \rep(X)$, equivalently if the Markov chain
$Y \markov \rep(X) \markov X$ holds. We write $\rep \suff \mathrm{id}$ for
``$\rep$ is sufficient'' and, more generally, $\rep \suff \rep'$ when
$\sigalg{\rep'} \subseteq \sigalg{\rep}$ up to $\Prob$-null sets and $\rep$ is
sufficient relative to $\rep'$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Sufficiency $\Leftrightarrow$ information equality]
\label{prop:suff-iff-mi}
$\rep$ is sufficient for $Y$ if and only if $\MI{Y}{\rep(X)} = \MI{Y}{X}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \cref{cor:dpi-rep}, equality in \eqref{eq:dpi-rep} holds iff
$Y \markov \rep(X) \markov X$, which is \cref{def:sufficiency}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Fisher--Neyman factorization]
\label{prop:fisher-neyman}
Suppose the conditional law of $X$ given $Y=y$ has density $f_y$ with respect
to a common $\sigma$-finite measure $\lambda$ on $\Xspace$. Then $\rep$ is
sufficient for $Y$ if and only if there exist measurable
$g_y : \Zspace_{\rep} \to [0,\infty)$ and $h : \Xspace \to [0,\infty)$ with
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:fisher-neyman}
  f_y(x) \;=\; g_y\!\big(\rep(x)\big)\, h(x)
  \qquad \lambda\text{-a.e., for every } y.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This is the factorization theorem \citep[Thm.~1.6.5]{lehmanncasella1998} with
the parameter index identified with the target value $y$ and the statistic
identified with the representation map $\rep$. The standard-Borel hypothesis
of \cref{ass:sbs} supplies the regular conditional laws the theorem requires.
\end{proof}

\cref{prop:fisher-neyman} is the operational test: a representation is
information-lossless for $Y$ exactly when the data likelihood depends on the
input only through $\rep$. The dependence on $x$ that factors into $h(x)$ is
the part a representation may freely discard --- it is the formal image of
``format that carries no predictive content.''

\subsection{Rate--distortion for lossy representations}
\label{subsec:ratedist}

When a representation is \emph{not} sufficient, \cref{cor:dpi-rep} is strict
and some predictive information is lost. The rate--distortion function
\citep[Ch.~10]{coverthomas2006} measures the minimal information a
representation must retain to keep a fidelity constraint, and converts the
information gap into a bound on achievable risk.

\begin{definition}[Rate--distortion function]
\label{def:ratedist}
For a fidelity (distortion) measure $d : \Xspace\times\Zspace_{\rep} \to
[0,\infty)$, the \emph{rate--distortion function} at level $D$ is
\[
  \ratedist \;\defeq\;
  \inf_{\,\rep \,:\, \Expect[d(X,\rep(X))]\le D}\, \MI{X}{\rep(X)},
\]
the infimum over (possibly stochastic) representations meeting the
distortion budget $D$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Lossy parity defect, previewed]
\label{rem:lossy-defect}
A representation that is sufficient for $Y$ sits at $D=0$ on the relevant
$Y$-fidelity axis: it loses no predictive information and \eqref{eq:dpi-rep}
is an equality. A lossy representation incurs a strictly positive
information gap $\MI{Y}{X}-\MI{Y}{\rep(X)} > 0$, and the rate--distortion
machinery bounds the resulting inflation of Bayes risk. The exact bound is the
rate--distortion corollary of the irrelevance theorem (T2-2); its quantitative
companion --- a Wasserstein/Fisher \emph{parity defect} computed against the
R4 round-trip data --- is \cref{sec:parity-defect}. We record here only that
the defect is governed by an information gap, so that ``how far from parity''
is a measurable quantity rather than a metaphor.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Information parity: operational versus absolute}
\label{subsec:parity-def}

We can now define the central object. Two grades are distinguished, because
the distinction is exactly guardrail~G3's ``parity is operational, not
absolute'' (paper §3.3), and the paper uses the weaker grade deliberately.

\begin{definition}[Information parity]
\label{def:parity}
Two representations $\rep, \rep'$ are in \emph{(operational) information
parity for $Y$}, written $\rep \parityeq \rep'$, if both are sufficient for
$Y$ in the sense of \cref{def:sufficiency}; equivalently, by
\cref{prop:suff-iff-mi},
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:parity}
  \MI{Y}{\rep(X)} \;=\; \MI{Y}{\rep'(X)} \;=\; \MI{Y}{X}.
\end{equation}
They are in \emph{absolute parity} if moreover $\sigalg{\rep} = \sigalg{\rep'}$
up to $\Prob$-null sets (their induced sub-$\sigma$-algebras agree, so each is
a measurable function of the other almost surely).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Absolute parity is strictly stronger]
\label{prop:abs-strict}
Absolute parity implies operational parity. The converse fails: there exist
$\rep \parityeq \rep'$ with $\sigalg{\rep} \ne \sigalg{\rep'}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $\sigalg{\rep}=\sigalg{\rep'}$ then each is a measurable function of the
other, so $\MI{Y}{\rep(X)}=\MI{Y}{\rep'(X)}$ and both equal $\MI{Y}{X}$ when
either is sufficient; this gives \eqref{eq:parity}. For the converse, let
$\rep' = (\rep, N)$ adjoin a component $N \indep (X,Y)$ that is independent
noise: $\rep'$ retains every predictive bit $\rep$ does, so
$\MI{Y}{\rep'(X)}=\MI{Y}{\rep(X)}$ and $\rep\parityeq\rep'$, yet
$\sigalg{\rep'} \supsetneq \sigalg{\rep}$. More to the point for the C-ladder:
$\ladderplus$ and $\ladder{4}$ can carry the same information about $Y$ while
generating different $\sigma$-algebras over byte-level inputs, which is
precisely what the round-trip data witness.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Which grade the paper uses, and why it matters]
\label{rem:which-parity}
The irrelevance theorem (T2-2) is stated against \emph{operational} parity
(\cref{def:parity}): that is all the Bayes-risk argument needs, since
\cref{prop:suff-iff-mi} ties operational parity to the equality case of the
data-processing inequality, and Bayes risk depends on a representation only
through the predictive information it retains (\cref{prop:disint}). Absolute
parity is sufficient but unnecessarily strong, and --- by
\cref{prop:abs-strict} --- it is the grade that \emph{fails} in practice: the
control $\ladderplus$ and the structured representation $\ladder{4}$ are
designed to be operationally parity (equal predictive content for $Y$) but are
not byte-identical, and the R4 round-trip recovery (\cref{sec:parity-defect})
quantifies the residual asymmetry $\sigalg{\ladder{4}} \ne
\sigalg{\ladderplus}$. The slogan ``parity is operational, not absolute'' is
therefore the formal statement that the central comparison rests on
\eqref{eq:parity} and not on $\sigma$-algebra equality; the gap between the
two grades is real, bounded, and quantified in \cref{sec:parity-defect}, never
assumed away.
\idealization{Operational parity \eqref{eq:parity} is an equality of mutual
informations under the (unknown but fixed) data law $\jlaw$. It is an
idealization in two senses made explicit downstream: (i) it presumes the two
representations are \emph{exactly} sufficient, whereas a constructed control
may be sufficient only up to a measured defect (\cref{sec:parity-defect}); and
(ii) the risk consequence it feeds (T2-2) presumes Bayes-optimality, which a
finite-capacity LLM does not attain (\cref{sec:bounded}).}
\end{remark}

\subsection{The irrelevance theorem (deferred to T2-2)}
\label{subsec:irrelevance}

With parity defined (\cref{def:parity}) and the data-processing inequality in
hand (\cref{lem:dpi,cor:dpi-rep}), the at-optimum invariance theorem is the
statement that \eqref{eq:parity} forces $\bayesrisk(\rep)=\bayesrisk(\rep')$.
The proof (sufficiency $\Rightarrow$ equal posteriors $\Rightarrow$ equal
Bayes risk, with a rate--distortion corollary for the lossy case of
\cref{rem:lossy-defect}) is the content of T2-2; the statement is recorded
here as a placeholder so that the foundations above have a visible target.

\begin{theorem}[Irrelevance at optimum --- placeholder]
\label{thm:irrelevance}
\emph{[Placeholder for T2-2.]} Under information parity
$\rep \parityeq \rep'$ (\cref{def:parity}), the Bayes risk is
format-invariant: $\bayesrisk(\rep) = \bayesrisk(\rep') = \bayesrisk(\mathrm{id})$.
\idealization{This statement assumes Bayes-optimality (the
infinite-capacity limit) and exact operational parity. A real frozen LLM
satisfies neither; \cref{sec:parity-defect} quantifies the parity defect and
\cref{sec:bounded} relaxes the capacity idealization. ``Parity is
operational, not absolute'' (preprint §3.3) is carried in here as the formal
caveat of \cref{rem:which-parity}.}
\end{theorem}
